Fall 2025

Rocket Body Epoxy Curing Oven

A custom-built, automated 120VAC curing oven designed to achieve even heat distribution for high-temperature composite rocket airframes — built for the Rice Eclipse competitive rocketry team.

Overview

The Challenge

Rice Eclipse is a competitive rocketry team at Rice University that designs and fabricates high-power rockets reaching speeds of Mach 2+ and skin temperatures up to 300°F. At those extremes, airframe integrity depends on composite tubes cured with high-temperature laminating epoxies — resins that demand sustained, precisely controlled heat to reach their full structural properties.

The team's existing oven was failing on every front: loose and disorganized wiring, malfunctioning temperature controls, and a broken tube-rotation mechanism that forced manual turning throughout multi-hour cure cycles. Commercial replacements with the required working volume (6″ × 6″ × 6 ft), programmable heating profiles, and reliable controls start at $2,000+ — far beyond Eclipse's budget. Without a functional oven, the team would be forced to use weaker epoxies, producing airframes incapable of surviving their target flight regime.

Our mandate was to design and build a new oven from scratch that could reliably reach 150°F+ with even heat distribution (±10°F across the tube face), feature an intuitive Arduino-driven user interface for setting temperatures and custom heating curves, and remain lightweight enough for two people to carry (≤75 lbs) — all while running on a standard 120VAC outlet and meeting NFPA 86 safety guidelines.

Process

Design Process

01

Requirements & Ideation

We began with extensive client interviews and background research to establish hard constraints: the oven had to reach at least 150°F on 120 VAC, fit under a storage table (≤ 1.5 × 1.5 × 8 ft), weigh under 75 lbs for a two-person carry, and comply with NFPA 86 industrial oven safety standards. We studied five aerospace-grade epoxy systems and their cure profiles to validate the temperature target, and surveyed commercial ovens to confirm that no viable solution existed within Eclipse's budget.

With constraints locked, the team ran two structured brainstorming sessions — generating 103 unique ideas across nine functional categories (heating elements, heat distribution, insulation, frame, UI, controls, wiring, power, and sensing). We used Pugh screening to systematically reduce these to 50 partial solutions, then assembled 30 complete system configurations via a morphological chart before a final weighted Pugh scoring round selected our winning architecture.

02

Thermal & Insulation Strategy

The heating subsystem was the highest-priority design decision. After Pugh screening eight heating element concepts — from space heaters to ceramic dishes — nichrome wire emerged as the clear winner for its high reliability, repairability, and precise thermal output. A relay-switched 120 VAC circuit powers the nichrome coil directly, avoiding lossy voltage conversion.

For insulation, we selected foil-lined ceramic fiber blanket — a combination that reflects radiant heat back into the chamber while providing excellent thermal resistance. The blanket is mounted to the wooden frame using custom L-bracket standoffs specifically designed to minimize conductive heat transfer to the plywood structure. A convection-assisted approach ensures even temperature distribution across the curing tube, targeting the ±10°F uniformity requirement critical for preventing localized undercuring or overcuring of the composite.

03

Controls & User Interface

The firmware is a C++ state machine running on an Arduino Mega, managing 11 distinct operational states — from mode selection and parameter input through preheating, active curing, and cancellation flows. Two K-type thermocouples read via MAX6675 amplifiers provide redundant temperature sensing; the firmware averages both readings and flags connectivity errors or dangerous thermal gradients (> 20°F differential).

The user-facing interface pairs a 20×4 LCD character display with a 4×4 membrane keypad. Two operation modes are supported: a "simple" mode for single-temperature timed cures, and a "heating curve" mode where users define per-hour temperature setpoints for complex epoxy schedules. Our design objective was for any operator to set a temperature in under 1 minute and program a full heating profile in under 8 minutes — targets validated through usability testing with six non-team-member participants.

04

Prototyping & Iteration

We followed a deliberate fidelity ramp. The low-fidelity prototype used construction paper for the casing, aluminum foil for insulation, and a pipe cleaner for the heating element — its sole purpose was to validate spatial relationships and component interfaces before committing to materials.

The medium-fidelity prototype was a miniature but fully functional oven: a 12 V power supply drove 30-gauge nichrome wire, a single thermocouple fed the Arduino, and the complete UI was operational. This build reached 180°F and validated our control logic, relay-switching behavior, and thermal response before we scaled to the full 120 VAC system. Through iteration, we replaced the originally planned stepper motor with a synchronous 120 VAC motor for the tube rotation system — gaining better reliability and inherent heat resistance — and swapped the initial button layout for a number pad after user testing revealed faster input times.

05

Final Assembly & Safety Integration

Every component in the final build was selected for high-temperature service — not just the insulation. Silicone heat-resistant wire replaced standard PVC-jacketed cable. Zirconia bearings support the tube rotation shaft. High-temperature JB Weld secures structural joints. Wherever the ceramic fiber insulation interfaces with the wooden frame, custom L-bracket standoffs create a thermal break that keeps the plywood well below its off-gassing threshold.

Safety was designed in at the system level: the interior foil liner is electrically grounded and the entire circuit is GFCI-protected, so if the foil — or a person — ever contacts the live nichrome element, the oven shuts off instantly. Ceramic insulators electrically isolate the nichrome wire from its supporting bolts. All low-voltage connections use WAGO lever-nut connectors and are clearly labeled, achieving a repairability score of 3+ on our custom UDS scale — meaning any engineering student with basic tools can diagnose and replace components without soldering.

Reflection

Key Takeaways

01

Multi-Voltage System Integration

Running 120 VAC power for the heating element and rotation motor alongside 5 V logic for the Arduino and sensors required careful isolation at every junction. Designing the relay-switching layer, grounding strategy, and GFCI protection as a unified safety architecture — rather than bolted-on afterthoughts — was the single most important decision for both reliability and operator safety.

02

Structured Evaluation Pays Off

Systematically screening 103 ideas through Pugh charts and morphological analysis felt slow at first, but it prevented us from locking onto an early favorite. The winning solution — nichrome coils with ceramic-fiber insulation and relay control — wasn't anyone's initial instinct; it emerged from the data. That rigor saved weeks of wasted prototyping on weaker concepts.

03

Designing for Legacy & Repairability

Rice Eclipse's composites team rotates members every year. Every wiring decision — WAGO connectors over solder joints, labeled connections, modular sensor boards — was made so that a future student who never met us can open the electronics bay, diagnose a fault, and swap a component with basic tools. Building for repairability is building for longevity.
