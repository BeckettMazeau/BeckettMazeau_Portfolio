Exponentially Greater Solar Energy Gained by Satellites in Polar Orbits

A 3-dimensional N-Body simulation of the solar system used to analyze how orbital tilt affects solar energy collection — using ray-traced sun visibility, Newton's law of universal gravitation, and Stefan-Boltzmann radiation modeling.

Overview

The Challenge

Solar energy has proven itself the most effective choice for powering the ever-growing number of satellites we use. The energy captured by a craft is dependent on the frequency, duration, and intensity of sunlight shining on it. To determine the most appropriate orbit for future satellites, this study used a 3-dimensional N-Body simulation to analyze the effects of different orbital tilts on energy collection, and thus determine the optimal angle for maximum energy capture.

The core challenge was determining not simply whether the sun was visible from the satellite at any given moment, but what percentage of the sun was visible — accounting for full and partial eclipses as well as the incremental rising and setting of the sun from the satellite's perspective. This required a ray-tracing based approach built on top of a full gravitational N-Body simulation incorporating all major bodies in the solar system.

The simulation was run across orbital tilts from 0° (ecliptic) to 90° (polar), with results recorded and analyzed in Excel. The finding: a clear exponential relationship between orbital polarity and total energy collected, with polar orbits capturing significantly more energy.

How It Works

Methodology

01

3D N-Body Gravitational Simulation

An N-Body system modeling all major celestial bodies in the solar system was built using a time-step approach, with gravity calculated every 1 simulated second via Newton's Law of Universal Gravitation: F = Gm₁m₂ / d², where G is 6.674×10⁻¹¹ N·m²/kg². A full solar system model (rather than just Earth and inner planets) was used to capture unique orbital resonances induced by planetary interactions.

02

Ray-Traced Sun Visibility

To accurately determine how much of the sun was visible to the satellite at any moment, 60,000 points were evenly distributed across the sun's surface using a golden-angle spiral. A ray was then traced from the satellite to each point, checking for body intersections. The fraction of unobstructed rays directly determined the effective solar exposure used in energy calculations.

03

Power Density Formula

Radiation density at the satellite was calculated using H₀ = (R²sun / D²) × Hsun, where D is the distance from the sun, Rsun is 695×10⁶ m, and Hsun is 64×10⁶ W/m² (Stefan-Boltzmann blackbody). This was then multiplied by the satellite's 4.5 m² panel area, a 20% panel efficiency factor, and the visible-point fraction to yield instantaneous power.

04

Experimental Design

Simulations were run at orbital tilts from 0° to 90° at 70 km altitude for 2 simulated years each. To simplify setup, all planets were initialized in alignment — a physically unrealistic but experimentally neutral condition, as any effect would be equal across all tests and thus produce no observable difference within the dataset. Maximum watts, minimum watts, orbital tilt, time elapsed, and total joules were recorded and analyzed in Excel.

05

2D Visual Component

The visual component of the simulation is for demonstration purposes only, rendered in 2D to reduce computational overhead. It represents only the x and z axes of each body. The satellite itself is not rendered, as at any meaningful simulation speed it would move too quickly to display usefully.

Reflection

Key Takeaways

01

Polar Orbits Are Exponentially Superior

The maximum energy collected was at 90° (polar), totaling 4.93×10¹⁰ joules, versus 4.29×10¹⁰ joules at 0° (ecliptic) — a difference of 6.4×10⁹ joules, or 101.5 W on average. The relationship is best modeled by a third-order polynomial, with gains accelerating as tilt increases past 45°. Any mission requiring high power — astronomical observation, terrestrial mapping — should use a perfectly polar orbit.

02

Ray Tracing Enables Accurate Partial Eclipses

A point-sampling ray-trace approach across 60,000 surface points gave a continuous, physically grounded measure of solar exposure — capturing not just total eclipses but the gradual onset and exit of partial ones. This level of fidelity would be impossible with a simple line-of-sight boolean check.

03

Simplifications Must Be Justified, Not Hidden

Several deliberate simplifications were made: modeling the satellite as a point, initializing planets in alignment, and omitting the y-axis from the visual. Each was explicitly reasoned through — the simulation's scale makes satellite size inconsequential, and the aligned-start condition cancels out uniformly across all test cases. Naming and justifying simplifications is as important as the physics itself.
