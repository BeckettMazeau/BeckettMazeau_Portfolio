Demonstrator of Phenomena at Lightspeed

A Processing simulation that visualizes the relativistic effects of approaching lightspeed — including Lorentz–FitzGerald length contraction and the relativistic Doppler shift — through an interactive 2.5D star field viewed from a rocket's reference frame.

Overview

The Challenge

Lightspeed travel is far beyond the reach of our current society — the energy needed to reach it is literally infinite, and thus theoretically impossible. We can, however, theoretically achieve significant fractions of lightspeed. It is at these significant fractions that our expectations of what reality should look like breaks down. Stars would become squished along the axis of motion, the Doppler effect would shift the colors we see them emit, and space itself would contract — producing a perceived increase in speed that would not be observable otherwise.

Talking about such concepts in the abstract does not accurately convey how we would experience near-lightspeed travel. This demonstrator aims to aid in visualizing the ways the universe's appearance and physics change as we approach c — specifically length contraction and the relativistic Doppler shift — through a real-time, interactive simulation.

While the libraries used to create this demonstration and the computer that runs it are extremely modern inventions, the equations that power it are much older: the Lorentz–FitzGerald contraction equation and the Doppler shift equation first described by Christian Doppler in 1842 (and extended to light by Hippolyte Fizeau).

How It Works

Simulation Design

01

2.5D Rendering Approach

While Processing's P3D engine was used to render the rocket itself, a 2.5D approach was used for the star field. To simulate forward motion from the reference frame of the rocket, a background of simulated stars moves across the screen rather than the rocket itself. Parallax was accounted for by decreasing each star's speed and displayed radius in proportion to its distance. This avoided the bulk and complexity of a full 3D engine while preserving the essential visual effects.

02

Length Contraction

Length contraction is modeled using the Lorentz–FitzGerald equation: L = L₀ √(1 − v²/c²). Because the simulation preserves constant time for the user, length contraction manifests as a perceived increase in speed — space contracts, stars appear closer, and parallax becomes more extreme. Stars are also visually squished along the horizontal axis, as contraction only acts in the direction of motion. The same contraction factor is applied to the x-component of each star's distance before it is used in Doppler calculations.

03

Relativistic Doppler Shift

Because screens cannot reproduce all spectral wavelengths in RGB, star colors are first converted to HSB. The hue is mapped to a wavelength using λ = 650 − ((250/270) × hue) (hue capped at 270 to avoid the ambiguous purple region). The wavelength is then shifted using the relativistic Doppler equation: λobs = λs √((1 + v/c) / (1 − v/c)). The shifted wavelength is converted back to an RGB value, preserving the original saturation and brilliance of the star.

04

Relative Star Velocity

Because the Doppler effect depends on the rate of change in distance — not simply speed along the x-axis — a full 3D relative velocity is calculated as the derivative of the distance formula with respect to time. The x-component of each star's distance is passed through the Lorentz contraction before being squared, ensuring the Doppler calculation is consistent with length contraction.

05

Interactive GUI Controls

The simulation is controlled via a LazyGui overlay. The primary user-facing controls are: speed/dX — the rocket's speed as a fraction of c; speed/timeScalar — simulation speed from 0 to 60 frames per second; speed/SimulatedRocketLocation — shifts the simulated location to better expose redshifting; and Fake Rocket Contraction — toggles a display of how the rocket would appear contracted from a stationary observer's reference frame (with an accuracy warning, as this situation cannot be faithfully represented in the simulation).

Reflection

Key Takeaways

01

Classical Equations Produce Surprising Visuals

The Lorentz–FitzGerald and relativistic Doppler equations are decades old, but applying them in real time to a moving star field produces genuinely counterintuitive results — stars compress into ellipses, the sky turns blue ahead and red behind, and the apparent speed of travel increases nonlinearly as v approaches c. The simulation makes the math visceral in a way that prose cannot.

02

Color Reproduction Has Hard Physical Limits

RGB screens cannot faithfully reproduce all spectral colors, which required an indirect HSB-to-wavelength mapping rather than a direct physics-to-pixel pipeline. The hue cap at 270 and the approximate conversion formula are principled workarounds, not oversights — and understanding why they are necessary deepens the physics of both light and display technology.

03

Acknowledge Known Inaccuracies Explicitly

The simulation has a documented inaccuracy: stars grow unexpectedly large as the rocket approaches them, because the simulated coordinate space is small and the x-component dominates the distance calculation. Rather than paper over it, the simulation acknowledges it directly in the write-up. Transparent documentation of known limitations is as important as the accuracy of what is correctly modeled.
