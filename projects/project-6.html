STIK-eNote

A pocketable, clamshell-style task tracking device built around the PocketBeagle. Always-on e-Paper for your priorities at a glance, a color TFT and mini keyboard when you need to edit, and a custom PCB tying it all together.

Overview

The Problem

Phones are bad task managers. Not because the apps are lacking, but because every time you pull one out to check your to-do list you get ambushed by notifications, messages, and whatever else is competing for your attention. The task list becomes an afterthought. I wanted a device that does exactly one thing: show me what I need to do, let me update it, and then get out of the way.

STIK-eNote is the answer to that. It's a clamshell device small enough to live in a pocket. When the lid is closed, a low-power e-Paper display persists your current task list at near-zero power draw. Open the lid and a magnetic sensor wakes the system, bringing up a 240x320 color TFT and a full QWERTY mini keyboard for editing. Close it again and everything shuts back down except the e-Paper.

The whole thing runs on a PocketBeagle (a stripped-down BeagleBone) with a custom PCB handling power gating, level shifting, and display driving. It's being designed for EDES 301 at Rice University.

Process

Design Process

01

Concept and Architecture

The starting point was a clear use case: a device that lives in your pocket, shows your tasks without powering up, and lets you edit them quickly when needed. That drove the clamshell form factor, the dual display approach (persistent e-Paper on the outside, interactive TFT on the inside), and the magnetic lid-detect mechanism that controls the power state.

The PocketBeagle was chosen for its small footprint, Linux support, and rich peripheral set (SPI, I²C, GPIO, PRU) without the overhead of a full BeagleBone. Early work focused on mapping every peripheral to specific PocketBeagle pins and confirming that the available buses could handle simultaneous SPI traffic to both displays plus I²C to the keyboard.

02

Schematic V1 — Roughing It Out

The first schematic revision was about getting every component onto a single page so I could see the full picture. It served its purpose for validating the circuit topology and confirming that the power gating, level shifting, and display driving approaches would work. But it was a mess: hundreds of wires crossing the page, no clear separation between subsystems, and not something I could hand to anyone else and expect them to follow.

More importantly, it wasn't structured in a way that would translate cleanly to a PCB layout. Routing from a single tangled sheet is a recipe for mistakes.

03

Schematic V2 — Industry Standard Formatting

V2 was a ground-up reformatting of the schematic into six sheets, each focused on a single functional block. Sheet 1 covers the PocketBeagle's pin assignments and top-level net routing. Sheets 2 through 6 handle the boost converter, hall effect power switch, TFT display, CardKB keyboard, and e-Paper display respectively.

The big change beyond formatting was the lid detection circuit. The original design used a reed switch, which worked but had issues with contact bounce causing occasional false wake/sleep transitions. V2 swaps it for a DRV5032FCDBZR latching hall effect sensor paired with a simple signal inverter. The inverter is necessary because the sensor's output polarity didn't match the PocketBeagle's wake-on logic level directly. The hall sensor is smaller, fully solid-state, and eliminates bounce entirely.

Pin labels now replace point-to-point wires between sheets, which makes the schematic readable for review (thanks to Erik Walsh for the review on this revision) and sets things up properly for PCB layout.

04

PCB Layout — Up Next

With the schematic properly organized, the next step is PCB layout in KiCad. The subsystem-per-sheet structure should make component placement and routing more manageable. Constraints are tight on board area since the whole thing needs to fit in a pocketable enclosure, so careful placement and probably a four-layer stackup will be necessary.

This section will be updated as the layout progresses.
